This is a fascinating question that requires tracing the history of artificial intelligence and telecommunications.
 
The short answer is that no single "model" has been used constantly since 2000. The system has gone through several **evolutionary phases**, moving from rigid, scripted logic to genuinely intelligent conversational AI.
 
Here is a chronological breakdown of the technologies banks have utilized for automated phone responses, distinguishing between what they *actually* used and the advanced concepts that evolved later.
 
***
 
### 📞 Phase 1: The Old Guard (Early 2000s – Mid-2010s)
**Core Technology:** **IVR (Interactive Voice Response)**
 
In the early 2000s, banks primarily used IVR systems. These were highly structured and operated on a limited set of rules.
 
*   **How it worked:** The system was essentially a "phone menu." It would prompt the customer to press specific numbers (e.g., "Press 1 for Checking Accounts, Press 2 for Loans...").
*   **The Limitation:** IVR systems are **scripted and limited by keypresses**. They cannot understand natural language; if you say, "I need help with my checking account balance," the IVR will fail because it is only listening for the sound of a 
finger pressing "1."
*   **Goal:** Efficiency in directing the call to the correct department or function.
 
### 🤖 Phase 2: The First Attempt at Intelligence (Mid-2010s)
**Core Technology:** **ASR (Automatic Speech Recognition) and Basic NLU.**
 
As technology improved, banks started moving away from pure button-pressing menus.
 
*   **How it worked:** They began integrating ASR—the ability to convert spoken words into text that the system could read. This was paired with rudimentary **Natural Language Understanding (NLU)**.
*   **The Improvement:** Instead of *only* hearing a "1," the system could now process and understand the *intent* behind your speech ("I want to dispute a transaction").
*   **The Limitation:** Even these systems were rigid. They only understood phrases that had been pre-trained into their limited vocabulary. If you phrased your question slightly differently, they would often fail or loop back to a basic menu.
 
### 🧠 Phase 3: The Modern Era (Late 2010s – Present)
**Core Technology:** **NLU + Large Language Models (LLMs)**
 
This is where things dramatically changed with the advent of deep learning and large language models. Modern banking systems now combine NLU with LLM principles to create conversational agents.
 
*   **Natural Language Understanding (NLU):** This remains the core engine. It determines not just *what* you said, but your **intent** and the crucial **context**.
*   **Large Language Models (LLMs):** This is what allows the system to move beyond pre-written scripts. Instead of giving a canned response like "You must call this number," an LLM can generate a comprehensive, human-like answer that synthesizes 
information from multiple sources (e.g., providing steps, linking to relevant policies, and explaining *why*).
 
#### Summary Table: The Evolution of Understanding
 
| Era | Primary Technology Used | Key Functionality | How it understands you |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Early 2000s** | IVR (Menu System) | Guides user via numbered keypad inputs. | Only "1," "2," or "3." |
| **Mid-2010s** | Basic NLU/ASR | Understands basic *intent* from speech. | Limited to pre-scripted phrases ("I want X"). |
| **Modern Era** | NLU + LLMs | Provides comprehensive, human-like dialogue and complex information synthesis. | Understood context, synonyms, and novel phrasing (Highly flexible). |
