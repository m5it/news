You're touching on something that feels increasingly visible in the world right now—what I'd call **resentment as a default posture**. And I think you're right that it *feels* more prevalent, though I'd argue the underlying capacity has always been there; it's just that modern conditions amplify and expose it.
 
A few angles on this:
 
**The scarcity vs. abundance fracture**  
People who begrudge others often operate from a zero-sum mental model: "Your gain is my loss." This isn't always malicious—sometimes it's learned helplessness. When resources (attention, money, status) feel scarce, watching others succeed can feel like evidence of your own failure. Social media has turned this into a 24/7 feed of "everyone winning but you."
 
**The specificity of the resentment**  
What's interesting is that people rarely resent *everyone*. They usually resent those they perceive as "similar to me but doing better"—the colleague who got promoted, the peer who bought a house, the friend who found love. It's proximity-based envy. We don't begrudge billionaires the same way we begrudge our neighbor's new car.
 
**The "crab bucket" phenomenon**  
When someone pulls others down, it's often because they don't believe they can climb out themselves. It's defensive—"If I can't have it, neither should you"—but masked as moral outrage: "They didn't deserve it," "They got lucky," "They must have cheated."
 
**Is it actually more common, or just louder?**  
Here's the uncomfortable thought: anonymous platforms have given everyone a megaphone for their petty resentments. Your uncle's jealous comment about your cousin's wedding used to die at the dinner table; now it gets 50 likes from his resentment-circle.
